## Sampling and Bias

### 1. Sample vs Population

**Population** = the full group you care about.
**Sample** = the smaller group you actually observe.

Example:

> You want to know the average height of all adults in Japan.

- Population: all adults in Japan
- Sample: 1,000 adults you measure

The goal of sampling is to use the sample to estimate something about the population.

But the key question is:

> Does the sample represent the population well?

A large sample is useful only if it is collected in a fair way.

---

## 2. Sampling Bias

**Sampling bias** happens when some members of the population are more likely to be included than others.

Example:

> You ask only gym members about average exercise habits.

That sample will probably overestimate how much people exercise, because gym members are not representative of everyone.

Bad conclusion:

> “Most people exercise 5 times per week.”

Better conclusion:

> “Among gym members, many people exercise often.”

Sampling bias means the data is already distorted before you analyze it.

---

## 3. Selection Bias

**Selection bias** is a broader idea: the way people or data points get selected affects the result.

Example:

> A company surveys only customers who stayed subscribed.

Then they conclude:

> “Customers are very satisfied.”

But they ignored people who canceled. The selection process removed unhappy customers.

Selection bias often appears when:

- participation is voluntary
- only available/easy-to-reach people are included
- failed cases are excluded
- the sample comes from one platform, school, company, or region

---

## 4. Survivorship Bias

**Survivorship bias** is a special type of selection bias.

It happens when you look only at the “survivors” and ignore those who failed or disappeared.

Classic example:

> Studying only successful startups to learn what makes startups succeed.

You may find patterns like:

> “Successful founders worked long hours.”

But many failed founders also worked long hours.

So the real lesson may not be “working long hours causes success.”

Another example:

> “This old building is very strong because it lasted 100 years.”

Maybe many weak buildings from the same time already collapsed. You are only seeing the ones that survived.

Survivorship bias often creates false success advice.

---

## 5. Sample Size Intuition

A larger sample usually reduces random noise.

Example:

Flipping a coin 10 times might give:

> 7 heads, 3 tails

That looks like 70% heads.

But flipping it 10,000 times will likely get closer to 50%.

So bigger sample size helps with **random variation**.

But it does **not** fix bias.

Example:

> You ask 100,000 Twitter/X users about political opinions.

That is a large sample, but it may still not represent the whole country.

**More biased data just gives you a more confident wrong answer.**

Important idea:

> Large sample size reduces noise, but not bias.

---

## Simple Mental Model

There are two common problems:

### Random error

The sample is fair, but small.

Example:

> You randomly ask 10 people.

The result may be unstable.

Solution:

> Increase sample size.

### Bias

The sample is collected unfairly.

Example:

> You ask only people in a luxury gym.

Even 10,000 answers may be misleading.

Solution:

> Improve sampling method.

---

## Example 1

Question:

> A university wants to know how students feel about cafeteria food. They survey students eating in the cafeteria at lunchtime.

Problem?

This sample misses:

- students who avoid the cafeteria because they dislike it
- students with different schedules
- students who bring their own food

So the result may be too positive.

This is sampling bias / selection bias.

---

## Example 2

Question:

> A website asks visitors, “Do you like our new design?”

Problem?

Only current visitors respond.

It misses:

- users who left because they hated the design
- users who could not use the site
- users who do not bother answering surveys

This can cause selection bias.

---

## Example 3

Question:

> Someone studies rich investors and concludes, “Taking big risks makes people rich.”

Problem?

They may be ignoring people who took big risks and lost everything.

That is survivorship bias.

---

## How to Spot Bias

Ask these questions:

1. **Who is missing from the sample?**
2. **Who was more likely to be included?**
3. **Did people choose themselves into the sample?**
4. **Are failures/dropouts/inactive users excluded?**
5. **Is the sample from only one place, app, school, company, or group?**
6. **Would the result change if missing people were included?**

The most useful question is:

> Who is not represented here?

## Key Takeaways

- **Population** is the full group you care about.
- **Sample** is the smaller group you observe.
- A sample must be representative, not just large.
- **Sampling bias** happens when the sample does not reflect the population.
- **Selection bias** happens when the selection process distorts the result.
- **Survivorship bias** happens when only successful/surviving cases are studied.
- More data reduces random error, but it does not fix bad sampling.

Most important sentence:

> More data does not fix biased data.
