# Music Theory Notes

These notes are based on the Udemy course **Music Theory Comprehensive**.

## General Principles

- Always trust your ear.
- Break the rules often.

## Terms

- **Accidentals**: Sharps and flats.
- **Intervals**:
  - **Octave**: A note to the next note with the same letter name, such as C to the next C.
    - Middle C is C4.
  - **Half step**: The smallest common distance between two notes in Western music, such as C to C# or E to F.
  - **Whole step**: Two half steps, such as C to D, C# to D#, Eb to F, E to F#, or B to C#.

- $3 \over 4$ means each bar has the length of three quarter notes.
- $6 \over 8$ means each bar has the length of six eighth notes.

## Scales

### Chromatic and Diatonic Settings

- **Diatonic**: Most notes stay within a key.
- **Chromatic**: Notes do not strictly follow a key.

### The Major Scale

#### Pattern

- W-W-H-W-W-W-H
  - W: Whole step
  - H: Half step

#### Examples

- C major: C D E F G A B C
- G major: G A B C D E F# G
- B major: B C# D# E F# G# A# B
- A major: A B C# D E F# G# A
- D major: D E F# G A B C# D
- E major: E F# G# A B C# D# E
- F major: F G A Bb C D E F

### Tonic, Scale Degrees, and Solfege

- The **tonic** is the first and last note of a scale. It is also the note the scale is named after.
  - In C major, the tonic is C.
- **Scale degrees** describe the position of a note in a scale, from 1 to 7.
  - In C major, G is scale degree 5.
- **Solfege**: Do Re Mi Fa Sol La Ti Do
  - **Movable do** defines Do as the tonic of the scale. The other syllables are named relative to that tonic.
    - Scale degree 1 is Do, 2 is Re, and so on.
  - **Fixed do** keeps Do as C, Re as D, and so on.

### Scale Degree Names

- 1: **Tonic**
- 2: **Supertonic**
  - Tends to resolve down to the tonic.
- 3: **Mediant**
  - Tends to resolve down to the tonic.
- 4: **Subdominant**
  - Can resolve up or down to the tonic.
  - Can also resolve down to the mediant or up to the dominant.
- 5: **Dominant**
  - Has a strong pull toward the tonic.
- 6: **Submediant**
  - Mostly neutral, but can resolve up to the tonic.
- 7: **Leading tone**
  - Has a strong pull upward to the tonic.

## Keys

### Tricks to Determine Major Keys

- If a key signature uses sharps:
  - Take the last sharp and move up a half step. That note is the key.
  - If that pitch is normally written with a sharp in the key signature, keep the sharp spelling.
- If a key signature uses flats:
  - One flat means F major.
  - Otherwise, take the second-to-last flat. That note is the key.
- No accidentals means C major.

### Minor Keys

- Minor keys are related to major keys.
- There are three common minor scales: natural minor, harmonic minor, and melodic minor.

#### Natural Minor

There are three useful ways to understand a natural minor scale.

##### Relative Minor

- The 6th note of a major scale is the tonic of its relative natural minor scale.
- C major: C D E F G A B C
- A minor: A B C D E F G A

##### Whole-Half Step Pattern

- W-H-W-W-H-W-W

##### Parallel Minor

- A parallel minor scale has the same tonic as the major scale.
- C major: C D E F G A B C
- C minor: C D Eb F G Ab Bb C

#### Harmonic Minor

- Raise the 7th note of the natural minor scale by a half step.
- This creates a leading tone, which is a half step below the tonic.

#### Melodic Minor

- Raise the 6th and 7th notes of the natural minor scale by a half step when ascending.
- When descending, the natural minor scale is usually used.

### Minor Key Limitations

- Natural minor does not have a leading tone a half step below the tonic.
- Because of this, the v chord does not pull as strongly to the i chord.
- In practice, the v chord is often changed to a major V chord by raising the 7th scale degree. This is the harmonic minor sound.

## Chords

### Triads

- Triads are chords with three notes.
- They are also called ternary harmony.

#### Major Triads

- Major triads use scale degrees 1, 3, and 5 from a major scale.
- C E G make up a C major chord.
- G B D make up a G major chord.

#### Diatonic Chord Progressions

- In a major scale, each diatonic triad is built by treating one scale degree as the root and stacking notes in thirds.
- In C major, the seven diatonic triads are:
  - C major: C E G
  - D minor: D F A
  - E minor: E G B
  - F major: F A C
  - G major: G B D
  - A minor: A C E
  - B diminished: B D F
    - Diminished sounds like an even more tense minor chord.
- The major-key triad pattern is: major, minor, minor, major, major, minor, diminished.

#### Inversions

- Inversions happen when the root note is not the lowest note.
- They can smooth transitions between chords.

#### Roman Numerals

- I ii iii IV V vi vii°
  - In C major, I is C major, ii is D minor, iii is E minor, IV is F major, V is G major, vi is A minor, and vii° is B diminished.
- Uppercase numerals mean major, lowercase numerals mean minor, and ° means diminished.

#### Major and Minor Triads

- The 3rd gives a triad its major or minor quality.
- Major triads have a major 3rd, which is two whole steps above the root.
- Minor triads have a minor 3rd, which is one and a half whole steps above the root.
- The 5th is the same in both major and minor triads.
- Diminished triads have a minor 3rd and a diminished 5th.
- Augmented triads have a major 3rd and an augmented 5th. They do not occur naturally in a major key.

#### 7th Chords

- 7th chords are four-note chords.
- Major triad + major 7th = major 7th chord (M7)
  - The major 7th is a half step below the octave.
- Minor triad + minor 7th = minor 7th chord (m7)
  - The minor 7th is a whole step below the octave.
- Major triad + minor 7th = dominant 7th chord (7)
  - Dominant 7th chords are common in blues.

#### Chord Progressions in Minor Keys

- The natural minor triad pattern is: i ii° III iv v VI VII.

#### Suspended Chords

- sus2: 1 2 5
- sus4: 1 4 5
  - The 3rd is replaced by the 2nd or 4th.
