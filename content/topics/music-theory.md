# Music Theory Notes

These notes are based on the Udemy course **Music Theory Comprehensive**.

## General Principles

- **Always trust your ear.** If it sounds good, it is good.
- **Break the rules often.** Music theory is descriptive, not prescriptive.

---

## Terminology

- **Accidentals**: Symbols indicating a pitch modification (e.g., Sharps, Flats, and Naturals).
- **Intervals**: The distance in pitch between two notes.
  - **Octave**: The interval between a note and the next note with the same letter name (e.g., from C to the next C). _Note: Middle C is denoted as C4._
  - **Half step (Semitone)**: The smallest common distance between two adjacent notes in Western music (e.g., C to C#, or E to F).
  - **Whole step (Tone)**: A distance of two half steps (e.g., C to D, or E to F#).

### Time Signatures

Time signatures are written as fractions. The top number indicates **how many** beats are in a bar, and the bottom number indicates **which note value** represents one beat.

| Signature       | Meaning                                                                                   |
| :-------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **$4 \over 4$** | Four quarter notes per bar. (Common time)                                                 |
| **$3 \over 4$** | Three quarter notes per bar. (Waltz time)                                                 |
| **$6 \over 8$** | Six eighth notes per bar. (Compound meter, often felt as two beats of three eighth notes) |

## Scales

### Chromatic and Diatonic Settings

- **Diatonic**: Notes stay strictly within a specific key.
- **Chromatic**: Notes do not strictly follow a specific key, utilizing all 12 notes.

### The Major Scale

The major scale follows a specific pattern of whole (W) and half (H) steps: **W - W - H - W - W - W - H**

**Examples of Major Scales:**

| Key         | Notes                              |
| :---------- | :--------------------------------- |
| **C major** | C - D - E - F - G - A - B - C      |
| **G major** | G - A - B - C - D - E - F# - G     |
| **D major** | D - E - F# - G - A - B - C# - D    |
| **A major** | A - B - C# - D - E - F# - G# - A   |
| **E major** | E - F# - G# - A - B - C# - D# - E  |
| **B major** | B - C# - D# - E - F# - G# - A# - B |
| **F major** | F - G - A - Bb - C - D - E - F     |

### Tonic, Scale Degrees, and Solfege

- **Tonic**: The first and last note of a scale. The scale is named after it (e.g., C is the tonic in C major).
- **Scale Degrees**: Describes the numerical position of a note in a scale, from 1 to 7 (e.g., G is scale degree 5 in C major).
- **Solfege**: Syllables used to name notes (Do, Re, Mi, Fa, Sol, La, Ti, Do).
  - **Movable Do**: "Do" is always the tonic of the current scale. Scale degree 1 is Do, 2 is Re, etc.
  - **Fixed Do**: "Do" is always C, "Re" is always D, regardless of the key.

### Scale Degree Names

Each degree of the scale has a traditional name and characteristic tendency:

| Degree | Name             | Tendency / Function                                                           |
| :----: | :--------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **1**  | **Tonic**        | The home base; the most stable note.                                          |
| **2**  | **Supertonic**   | Tends to resolve down to the tonic.                                           |
| **3**  | **Mediant**      | Tends to resolve down to the tonic.                                           |
| **4**  | **Subdominant**  | Can resolve up/down to the tonic, down to the mediant, or up to the dominant. |
| **5**  | **Dominant**     | Has a strong harmonic pull toward the tonic.                                  |
| **6**  | **Submediant**   | Mostly neutral, but can resolve up to the tonic.                              |
| **7**  | **Leading Tone** | Has a very strong pull upward to resolve to the tonic.                        |

## Keys

### Tricks to Determine Major Keys from Key Signatures

- **Sharps**: Take the last sharp (furthest right) and move up a half step. That note is the key. _(Note: If that pitch is normally written with a sharp in the key signature, keep the sharp spelling, e.g., F# major)._
- **Flats**: Take the second-to-last flat. That note is the key. _(Exception: One flat means F major)._
- **No accidentals**: C major.

### Minor Keys

Minor keys are closely related to major keys. There are three common minor scales: Natural, Harmonic, and Melodic minor.

#### 1. Natural Minor

There are three ways to understand a natural minor scale:

1. **Relative Minor**: The 6th note of a major scale is the tonic of its relative natural minor scale (they share the same key signature).
   - _Example:_ C major (C D E F G A B C) $\rightarrow$ A minor (A B C D E F G A)
2. **Step Pattern**: **W - H - W - W - H - W - W**
3. **Parallel Minor**: Shares the same tonic as the major scale, but lowers the 3rd, 6th, and 7th degrees.
   - _Example:_ C major (C D E F G A B C) $\rightarrow$ C minor (C D Eb F G Ab Bb C)

#### 2. Harmonic Minor

- Raise the 7th note of the natural minor scale by a half step.
- This creates a leading tone (a half step below the tonic), giving a stronger resolution to the tonic.

#### 3. Melodic Minor

- Raise the 6th and 7th notes of the natural minor scale by a half step when _ascending_.
- When _descending_, revert to the natural minor scale.

### Minor Key Limitations

- **The Problem**: Natural minor lacks a leading tone (it has a whole step below the tonic instead of a half step). Because of this, the minor **v** chord does not pull as strongly to the minor **i** chord.
- **The Solution**: In practice, the **v** chord is often changed to a major **V** chord by raising the 7th scale degree of the key. This utilizes the **harmonic minor** sound.

## Chords

### Triads

Triads are chords built with three notes stacked in thirds (also called ternary harmony).

- **The 3rd** determines if a triad is major or minor.
- **The 5th** is typically the same in both major and minor triads.

| Triad Quality  | Construction                                     | Example (C Root) |
| :------------- | :----------------------------------------------- | :--------------- |
| **Major**      | Root + Major 3rd (2 whole steps) + Perfect 5th   | C - E - G        |
| **Minor**      | Root + Minor 3rd (1.5 whole steps) + Perfect 5th | C - Eb - G       |
| **Diminished** | Root + Minor 3rd + Diminished 5th                | C - Eb - Gb      |
| **Augmented**  | Root + Major 3rd + Augmented 5th                 | C - E - G#       |

_(Note: Augmented triads do not occur naturally in a major key.)_

### Diatonic Chord Progressions

In a major scale, diatonic triads are built by stacking notes in thirds using only the notes of the scale. The pattern of chord qualities in _every_ major key is:
**Major - minor - minor - Major - Major - minor - diminished**

**Roman Numeral Analysis (e.g., C Major):**
Uppercase numerals mean major, lowercase numerals mean minor, and ° means diminished.

| Numeral  | Chord   | Notes     | Quality                  |
| :------: | :------ | :-------- | :----------------------- |
|  **I**   | C major | C - E - G | Major                    |
|  **ii**  | D minor | D - F - A | minor                    |
| **iii**  | E minor | E - G - B | minor                    |
|  **IV**  | F major | F - A - C | Major                    |
|  **V**   | G major | G - B - D | Major                    |
|  **vi**  | A minor | A - C - E | minor                    |
| **vii°** | B dim   | B - D - F | diminished (tense minor) |

\*In a natural minor key, the triad pattern shifts to: **i - ii° - III - iv - v - VI - VII\***

### Inversions

- Inversions occur when the root note is not the lowest note (the bass).
- They are used to create smoother, more musical transitions between chords in a progression.

### 7th Chords

7th chords are four-note chords (a triad plus a 7th note).

- **Major 7th (M7)**: Major Triad + Major 7th (half step below the octave).
- **Minor 7th (m7)**: Minor Triad + Minor 7th (whole step below the octave).
- **Dominant 7th (7)**: Major Triad + Minor 7th (very common in blues and jazz, creating tension).

### Suspended Chords

Suspended (sus) chords replace the 3rd degree with either a 2nd or a 4th, creating an unresolved, floating sound.

- **sus2**: Root - 2nd - 5th (e.g., C - D - G)
- **sus4**: Root - 4th - 5th (e.g., C - F - G)
